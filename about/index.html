Why I ended up here.

I came into hotel work through automation, not hospitality school.

That is probably worth saying plainly. I am not pretending to be a lifelong hotel operator. My background is building software and systems, then using that lens inside messy real-world workflows.

When I started doing consulting work with hotel teams, I expected more of the work to be "AI." Some of it is. But the recurring theme was more basic and more important: smart people were still spending too much time assembling the same reports by hand.

The useful question usually is not "how do we add AI?" It is "why is a person still rebuilding this same operating view every morning?"

That question is where most of my best projects start.

What I am actually looking for.

Most hotel teams already have plenty of data. The harder part is getting from the source reports to the morning view consistently, cleanly, and early enough for the team to use it.

Manual assembly

The repeated steps between source reports and the finished view.

Pulling exports, copying numbers, cleaning files, updating tabs, checking exceptions, saving versions, and sending the summary. This is often where the time leak lives.

Useful output

The format the team will actually use.

Sometimes that is a dashboard. Sometimes it is a Google Sheet, a morning email, a file dropped into the right folder, or a brief that tells the team what to review first.

AI after the basics

Interpretation works better after the workflow is reliable.

AI can help summarize changes, flag exceptions, and point people to the right questions. But it works best when the underlying reporting workflow is already consistent.

How a first project usually works.

I try to keep the first engagement narrow enough that everyone can see value quickly. The goal is not to boil the ocean. It is to prove the workflow can run without someone rebuilding it by hand every morning.

1
We map the actual workflow, including the awkward parts.

Which reports exist, where they come from, who pulls them, what gets copied or cleaned, what breaks, and who depends on the final output.

2
We automate the assembly from what already exists.

Exports, spreadsheets, scheduled emails, folders, and existing reports are usually enough to start. Direct PMS access is not usually required for the first useful version.

3
We make the output boringly reliable.

The right win is an output that shows up on time, in the right format, with the numbers and exceptions the team expects to see.

4
Then we add interpretation where it helps.

Once the operating view is clean, AI can help explain what changed, what looks unusual, and what the team should review first.

A good first project should feel practical. If I do not think there is a fast, useful automation opportunity in the workflow you show me, I would rather say that early than sell you a project that turns into a science fair.

A few honest notes.

Not magic

I do not sell AI as a shortcut around understanding the workflow.

The workflow has to be mapped. The source reports have to be understood. The output has to match how the team actually works.

Not precious

I am happy when the best output is simple.

If an automated email or updated spreadsheet solves the problem better than a custom app, that is the answer. Adoption matters more than polish for its own sake.

Not theoretical

I like building the first working version quickly.

It is easier to improve a real workflow than debate a hypothetical one. Start narrow, make it work, then decide what deserves to be expanded.

Start with the workflow your team keeps rebuilding.

Tell me what gets pulled, cleaned, copied, checked, or summarized every morning. I will tell you honestly whether it looks like a good fit for a focused automation pilot.
